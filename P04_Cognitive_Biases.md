### General techniques to reduce cognitive bias

- Slow down decisions involving significant money, health, or education.
- Separate facts, assumptions, and opinions.
- Use checklists or predefined decision criteria.
- Seek informed disagreement rather than only agreement.
- Revisit important decisions after emotions have cooled.

No one eliminates cognitive biases completely. The goal is to recognize when they are likely to affect judgment and build habits that reduce their influence.

# DeepSeek Response

Here are seven cognitive biases that frequently shape decisions in Indian households, with everyday examples, reasons, and ways to counter them.

---

**1. Status Quo Bias**  
Preferring things to stay the same, even when change could be better.  
*Example:* Insisting a child pursue engineering or medicine simply because “everyone in our family/community does it,” or always investing only in fixed deposits without exploring other options.  
*Why it happens:* Loss aversion, societal safety, and fear of judgment. The familiar feels less risky, and tradition carries weight.  
*What to do:* Ask, “If I were making this choice for the first time, with no past history, what would I pick?” Compare alternatives on their current merits, not past precedent.

---

**2. Authority Bias**  
Overweighing the opinion of an elder or authority figure without independent evaluation.  
*Example:* The family patriarch decides all financial investments, and no one questions it, even when returns are poor or the risk is unsuitable.  
*Why it happens:* Deep cultural deference to elders, joint-family hierarchy, and the belief that age equals wisdom.  
*What to do:* Normalize shared decision-making. Each member can bring data or a second professional opinion to a family discussion, gently decoupling respect for the person from automatic acceptance of every decision.

---

**3. Sunk Cost Fallacy**  
Continuing a course of action because of past investment, not future value.  
*Example:* Forcing a child through years of IIT coaching they despise, because “we’ve already spent lakhs.” Or staying in an unhappy marriage because of dowry and wedding expenses.  
*Why it happens:* Aversion to admitting a mistake, loss of face, and the false belief that persevering will “recover” spent resources.  
*What to do:* Reframe the decision: “If I had invested nothing so far, would I still choose this path right now?” Focus only on future costs and benefits, and give yourself permission to cut losses.

---

**4. Social Proof (Bandwagon Effect)**  
Doing something because relatives, neighbours, or the community are doing it.  
*Example:* Buying a specific car, hosting a destination wedding, or pressuring a child into the science stream simply because “Sharma ji’s son did it.”  
*Why it happens:* Intense desire for social belonging, fear of standing out (log kya kahenge), and equating conformity with status.  
*What to do:* Pause and list your family’s unique needs and values. Ask, “Would this decision make sense if no one else could see it?” Disconnect the choice from the herd’s stamp of approval.

---

**5. Anchoring Bias**  
Latching onto the first number or piece of information encountered.  
*Example:* In a wedding negotiation, the first dowry or salary demand anchors the entire discussion. At the sabzi mandi, the vendor’s initial high price distorts your idea of a fair deal.  
*Why it happens:* Mental shortcuts; we insufficiently adjust away from the anchor, especially under time pressure or social awkwardness.  
*What to do:* Research a realistic range beforehand. When a number is thrown at you, consciously set your own reference point and ask, “What independent data do I have to judge this offer?”

---

**6. Confirmation Bias**  
Seeking and remembering information that supports what you already believe.  
*Example:* Parents who believe “only a government job is secure” notice every news story about private-sector layoffs but ignore private-sector successes or government job struggles. Families watch only one ideological news channel to validate their political views.  
*Why it happens:* It feels comfortable to be right; questioning beliefs creates mental friction. Echo chambers reinforce it.  
*What to do:* Deliberately expose yourself to a credible opposing view. Before deciding, ask, “What would have to happen for me to change my mind?” Then check if that evidence exists.

---

**7. Present Bias**  
Favouring immediate rewards over much larger future gains.  
*Example:* Draining retirement savings for a lavish wedding or festival celebration; buying the latest phone on EMI while neglecting a child’s education fund.  
*Why it happens:* The present moment is vivid and emotionally charged. Cultural expectations reward visible, immediate generosity, while distant needs feel abstract.  
*What to do:* Make the future feel concrete: “What will our life look like in 10 years if we make this choice versus that one?” Automate savings *before* money reaches your spending account, and treat long-term goals as non-negotiable expenses.

# Claude Response

Here are seven biases that show up a lot in Indian household decisions:

**1. Anchoring Bias** _Example:_ Bargaining at a sabzi mandi or during a wedding shopping trip — the first price quoted becomes the reference point for "how good" the final deal feels, even if it's still overpriced. _Why it happens:_ The brain leans on the first number it hears instead of evaluating true value. _Fix:_ Research typical prices beforehand; ignore the seller's opening number and set your own reference point.

**2. Social Proof / Herd Mentality** _Example:_ Buying a particular insurance policy or investing in a stock because "sab log le rahe hain" (everyone's buying it), or choosing a college because relatives' kids went there. _Why it happens:_ Following the group feels safer and reduces the effort of independent research. _Fix:_ Ask "would I choose this if no one else had?" Separate popularity from suitability.

**3. Loss Aversion** _Example:_ Keeping money in a low-interest savings account or gold instead of mutual funds, because the fear of losing principal outweighs the appeal of higher returns. _Why it happens:_ Psychologically, losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. _Fix:_ Reframe decisions in terms of long-term opportunity cost, not just immediate safety.

**4. Sunk Cost Fallacy** _Example:_ Continuing to fund a struggling family business or a child's uninterested engineering degree because "already itna paisa laga diya hai" (we've already spent so much). _Why it happens:_ People hate admitting past investment was wasted, so they throw good money after bad. _Fix:_ Ask "knowing what I know now, would I start this today?" — ignore past spend, decide fresh.

**5. Authority Bias** _Example:_ Following a doctor's or elder relative's advice on health, finances, or career without question, even when it conflicts with better information. _Why it happens:_ Deference to age, position, or credentials is deeply culturally reinforced. _Fix:_ Respect the source, but still verify — "trust but verify" applies even to elders and experts.

**6. Confirmation Bias** _Example:_ Only watching news channels or WhatsApp forwards that match existing political or religious views, reinforcing family arguments. _Why it happens:_ It's mentally comfortable to seek information that confirms what we already believe. _Fix:_ Deliberately read/watch one credible opposing source before forming an opinion.

**7. Status Quo Bias** _Example:_ Sticking with the same family doctor, bank, or even career path for decades, not because it's optimal, but because switching feels risky or effortful. _Why it happens:_ Change requires energy and carries uncertainty, so the brain defaults to "what we know." _Fix:_ Periodically audit long-standing choices — ask "if I were choosing fresh today, would I pick this again?"

**General reduction strategy:** slow down big decisions, write down the reasoning before deciding, and get an outside perspective from someone with no stake in the outcome — biases thrive on speed and social pressure, not scrutiny.
