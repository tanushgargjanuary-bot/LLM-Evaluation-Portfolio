### 1. Behavior follows incentives

Ask: **"What does this person gain?"**

People seek rewards (money, approval, comfort, belonging, meaning) and avoid costs (effort, embarrassment, rejection, uncertainty).

Example:

- Someone says they value health but never exercises.
- Immediate comfort outweighs distant health benefits.

Look for actual incentives, not stated values.

---

### 2. Emotions come first; logic often comes second

The brain often produces a feeling before a justification.

Examples:

- Buying an expensive phone because it "feels right," then citing technical specs.
- Disliking someone first, then noticing only their flaws.

When people argue, they are often defending emotions or identity rather than evaluating evidence.

---

### 3. Humans are deeply social

Belonging was historically essential for survival.

This explains:

- Peer pressure
- Fashion trends
- Tribal politics
- Fear of public embarrassment
- Desire for recognition

Much behavior is signaling:

- Competence
- Wealth
- Kindness
- Intelligence
- Loyalty
- Uniqueness

People are influenced by what others appear to believe or do.

---

### 4. Identity is powerful

People protect their self-image.

"I made a mistake" is easier than "I am the kind of person who makes this mistake."

When facts threaten identity:

- Evidence is scrutinized more harshly.
- Contradictory information is discounted.
- Beliefs become more entrenched.

Changing identity is often harder than changing opinions.

---

### 5. Habits dominate daily life

Much behavior is automatic.

Habit loop:

1. Cue
2. Routine
3. Reward

Changing environments often changes behavior more effectively than relying on willpower.

---

### 6. Attention is limited

People cannot process everything.

The brain prioritizes:

- Novelty
- Threat
- Emotion
- Personal relevance

This explains:

- Clickbait
- Rumors
- Doomscrolling
- Forgetfulness
- Missed details

---

### 7. Cognitive biases are useful shortcuts

Biases are mental heuristics, not simply flaws.

High-impact examples:

- **Confirmation bias:** notice evidence supporting existing beliefs.
- **Availability heuristic:** memorable events seem more common.
- **Loss aversion:** losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good.
- **Anchoring:** first numbers strongly influence later judgments.
- **Fundamental attribution error:** explain others by personality but ourselves by circumstances.

---

### 8. Short-term rewards often beat long-term goals

Humans discount the future.

This explains:

- Procrastination
- Overspending
- Poor diets
- Addiction
- Failure to save

Immediate rewards usually outweigh larger delayed benefits unless the environment compensates.

---

### 9. Stress changes thinking

Under stress people become more likely to:

- Use habits instead of deliberate reasoning.
- Focus on immediate threats.
- Become emotionally reactive.
- Narrow attention.

Many behaviors reflect current cognitive load rather than stable personality.

---

### 10. People are poor at knowing why they act

People can accurately report experiences but are often less accurate about the true causes of their behavior.

Instead of asking only **"What did they say?"**, also ask:

- What were the incentives?
- What emotions were present?
- What identity was involved?
- What habits existed?
- What constraints shaped the choice?

## Practical mental checklist

When someone's behavior seems confusing, ask:

1. What are they trying to gain or avoid?
2. What emotion is driving them?
3. What identity are they protecting?
4. What social pressures exist?
5. What habits are involved?
6. What biases could be influencing them?
7. What constraints or incentives does the environment create?

These questions explain a large fraction of everyday decisions, conflicts, persuasion, and relationships more reliably than focusing on personality labels alone.

# DeepSeek Response

Here's the 20% that explains 80%:

---

**1. We're predictably irrational.**
We don't decide based on logic, then feel. We feel first, then rationalize. Most decisions are emotional, justified after the fact. If you want to influence behavior, aim for emotion, not just facts.

---

**2. The brain's #1 job is safety, not accuracy.**
We're wired to:
- Seek pleasure, avoid pain
- Conserve energy (mental and physical)
- Seek certainty and familiarity

This explains procrastination, resistance to change, sticking with bad situations, and why habits beat willpower. People don't resist because they're difficult—they resist because the unknown registers as a threat.

---

**3. Every behavior answers a need.**
Behaviors that look dysfunctional (people-pleasing, avoidance, aggression) are strategies to meet core needs: safety, belonging, significance, autonomy. To change a behavior, replace it with a better way to meet the same need.

---

**4. We're social creatures before rational ones.**
The brain processes social pain (rejection, shame, exclusion) like physical pain. This drives:
- Conformity (we follow the group even when we know it's wrong)
- Status-seeking (much of what we buy and do signals position)
- People agreeing in public but privately disagreeing

Core drivers: Belonging matters more than being right.

---

**5. The mind has two systems.**
- **System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, pattern-matching. Runs 95%+ of the day.
- **System 2:** Slow, deliberate, logical. Drains energy. Rarely kicks in unless forced.

Implication: Most behavior is automatic. If you want to understand someone, look at their environment and habits, not their stated intentions. To change behavior, make the desired action the easy/default option.

---

**6. Cognitive biases that run everything.**
- **Confirmation bias:** We seek evidence for what we already believe.
- **Fundamental attribution error:** My failures are situational; yours are your character.
- **Loss aversion:** Losing hurts roughly twice as much as winning feels good. This is why "don't miss out" and "protect what you have" work.
- **In-group / out-group:** We favor our "tribe" and distrust outsiders—instantly and unconsciously.

---

**7. What drives lasting behavior.**
We move toward pleasure and away from pain, but we move *fastest* when:
- The payoff is immediate
- The cost is low
- The path is clear
- The social environment supports it

If someone isn't doing something, check which of those is missing. The solution is rarely "educate them more."

---

**8. Identity governs action.**
People act consistently with who they believe they are. Telling someone "you should exercise" hits System 2 and fails. When they see themselves as "an active person," exercise becomes automatic. Self-story is the operating system of behavior.

---

**Practical integration:**
- Want to understand an action? Ask: what need is it meeting, and what environment is triggering it?
- Want to change behavior? Change the path (environment), the reward structure (immediate payoff/pain), or the self-story (identity)—not the logic.
- Want to persuade? Speak to safety, belonging, and status. Make it easy and familiar. Make them feel it first; logic comes second.

# Claude Response

Here's the compressed version — the mental models that explain most of what you'll see people do:

**1. People act to reduce discomfort, not maximize logic.** Most behavior is driven by avoiding pain (boredom, anxiety, rejection, uncertainty) more than pursuing gain. When someone procrastinates, snaps, or avoids a conversation, ask "what discomfort are they dodging?" before "what are they trying to achieve?"

**2. Behavior is mostly habit, not decision.** Roughly 40% of daily actions are automatic routines triggered by context, not conscious choice. If you want to change someone's behavior (including your own), change the environment/trigger, not the willpower.

**3. We decide emotionally, then justify rationally.** People reach a gut conclusion fast, then build logical-sounding reasons afterward. This is why arguing facts rarely changes minds — the real lever is usually feeling heard first.

**4. Loss looms larger than gain.** Losing $100 hurts roughly twice as much as gaining $100 feels good. This explains risk-aversion, why people stay in bad jobs/relationships, and why "don't lose X" pitches beat "gain X" pitches.

**5. We are what we've said and done before (consistency bias).** People act to stay consistent with their past behavior and self-image, even when it's no longer rational. This explains stubbornness, sunk-cost thinking, and why small early commitments predict big later ones.

**6. We copy the people around us.** Humans default to social proof — "what are others doing" — especially under uncertainty. This explains trends, peer pressure, and why testimonials/crowds persuade more than arguments.

**7. We judge others by character, ourselves by circumstance** (fundamental attribution error). When someone else messes up, we think "they're careless." When we mess up, we think "traffic was bad." Remembering this defuses a lot of unnecessary conflict.

**8. Under stress, the brain downgrades to fight/flight/freeze.** Logic, patience, and empathy are the first things to go offline when someone feels threatened. A "difficult person" is often just a stressed nervous system, not a bad character.

**9. Everyone is quietly asking "do I matter here?"** Most social friction traces back to status and belonging — feeling respected, seen, or excluded. A lot of "irrational" reactions make sense once you spot the status threat underneath.

If you only kept three: **people avoid discomfort, justify decisions after making them emotionally, and act to protect their self-image and status.** That trio explains a surprising amount of what looks confusing on the surface.
